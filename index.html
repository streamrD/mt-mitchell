A Gift, Revisited

Photographs & writing · 2016–2026

Scroll
April, 2026

This is my fourth time hiking on Mt. Mitchell this month. I hadn't been up here since 2024, just before Helene. I need this place right now. I come to these mountains to see again.

Driving up the Blue Ridge Parkway, even now you see trees leaning against each other like matchsticks, swaths of hillsides stripped bare by mudslides, evidence of a hurricane where a hurricane should not be. The spruce-fir ecosystem of the Southern Appalachians is more fragile than it appears, clinging to these peaks like a relic of the last Ice Age. It exists nowhere else at this latitude. It does not recover quickly. What a storm removes in a night may take a century to return.

And yet, stand still long enough and you feel permanence beneath the fragility. These Black Mountains are among the oldest on earth, worn down over hundreds of millions of years by nothing more than water, time, and wind. A drop, a rivulet, repeated again and again, multiplied by numbers too large for a human mind to hold. Helene was catastrophic by any human measure. On the scale by which these mountains keep time, it was barely a tremor.

When you walk into these spruce-fir forests, the smell of Christmas fills your nose — elevated above any nostalgia — resinous, bracing, clean. Then you notice the sound — more accurately, the absence of it. Today as I entered I tried to locate something I was hearing, but realized it was my own blood rushing in my head. The silence presses in on you from all sides. Even the birds are different here. They converse with this silence, ask it questions, listen.

This is what a cathedral wants to be. The only proper word for it is holy.

At some point the wind will announce itself. When it lifts and moves through this canopy, it invokes vastness, openness, distance in a low, continuous surge that arrives from everywhere at once and cannot be localized. The sound of an ocean, but overhead as well as below.

I've walked these paths at the height of summer's groggy heat, the air thick and close even at this elevation. I've seen the transformation a sudden rain brings — moss soaked through, the rocky trail now a stream — wet shoes, wet clothes — the whole forest snapping back to life. I spent a night on a high, exposed ridge here in a torrential storm, thunder crashing all around, the wind roaring, collapsing my tent on top of me over and over all night long so I wasn't sure I'd be hiking out in the morning. Mitchell is not one place. It is many places occupying the same coordinates. I've learned to come here without expectations and let it show me what I need.

What it gave me today is clouds.

Whatever work they had been doing last night, by sunrise they had settled down into the valleys and gone solid — a massive expanse of white that my eye first registered as a churning sea, eerily frozen like a photograph. The peaks of the next ranges poked up through the cloud layer like islands off the coast of an otherworldly shore. Where the white ended it merged with an even vaster field of blue receding into an open horizon — depth without terminus — registering as water just as easily as sky — the distant views my soul has craved. The clouds had rounded themselves over the nearest summits like thick blankets, tucked in close. There was no wind to speak of — just that silence.

500 million years ago, the rock under my boots was once seafloor — the sediment of a vanished ocean called the Iapetus, pressed and folded and lifted over hundreds of millions of years into the peaks I'm walking now. The Appalachians rose out of the sea. They are the sea, reconstituted. Across millions of years, the ocean has been focused, working, finding a new way to see itself: me, standing on this ridge, watching clouds fill the valleys below like the water that was the only thing here before. And you, reading these words. We are what the sea became when it had time and space to think.

I come to these mountains to see again.

Over the course of the morning, the clouds begin to tease themselves apart. By the time I am driving back down the Parkway to Asheville, they have resumed their familiar roles — discrete, evanescent, casually drifting and draping the mountainsides. The world returning to itself. On the way down I heard the wind again, or maybe I just remembered it — that hushed roar moving through the firs like the sound of the waves. In landlocked mountains like these, maybe it isn't strange at all. Maybe this is just the mountain remembering out loud.

Nine years earlier

July, 2017

Two years ago I hiked for the first time up one of Mt. Mitchell's long backcountry trails to the summit. It felt like I had been granted entrance to another world. I was inspired to write the words below, calling this experience "A Gift." Both years since then Mt. Mitchell has given me gifts that defy a description in the words or the combination of words and photos or videos I often try to use. I just got back from another adventure there and find myself reminiscing on how we form soul-level connections to those places that have the most to teach us.

I had arrived several times before by bike and a couple by car to the top of Mt. Mitchell, the crown of the Black Mountains and the highest point east of the Mississippi. This was the first time I got there powered by my own two feet. With my 50th birthday a couple months away, I found myself magnetically drawn here as if to balance some force I couldn't comprehend. It was not the first time I would climb that majestic peak this fall.

While milling around among the crowds of people who had driven to the top so they could take photos for Facebook, I spotted a side trail and followed it down the back of the mountain. As it meandered over rocky terrain away from the crowds, at some magical point I realized I was surrounded by the kind of silence that is defined by the absence of human noise. I was still under that dense spruce-fir canopy, but this kind of silence opened up space all around me. I had been craving this, without realizing it until that moment. I kept going. Soon I caught the faint, tantalizing sound of running water. I would have to venture off the trail to find it, and that's just what I was going to do.

The sound got louder as I crouched and pushed aside branches.

Finally, the woods opened, and my ears were filled with the glorious chaos of a majestic crashing waterfall. Soaked with sweat from 10 miles of uphill hiking, I looked down and in that moment saw my clothes as a burden, the only remaining obstacle between my soul and the perfect alignment with nature I had been closing in on all day. I left them in a pile. I stepped carefully over mossy rocks that had existed for millions of years only to find their way to those exact spots. What similarly mysterious forces smoothed my rough edges and brought me here to take note of them in this moment? I felt exhilaration as I slipped into that cold mountain stream.

One day I might try to find my way back to this spot, but I knew without a doubt that this moment was as utterly unique as it was perfect. And that it was a gift for me alone.
